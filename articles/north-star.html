Metric Definition & Strategy

The One Number a Whole Company Steers By

Every team was shipping, every dashboard was green — and still no one could say whether any of it was making customers stay. This is the story of the one metric built to answer that, and to make a whole organisation steer by it.

Atif Salman  ·  Metric Definition & Strategy · On-Demand Delivery  ·  July 2026

Here's a frustration familiar to anyone who has worked inside a large product organisation. Every team is busy. Every team has its metrics and hits most of its targets. Revenue is climbing, the dashboards are green — and yet, if you stop and ask the most basic question, are we actually building the things that make customers stay?, nobody can quite draw the line. A team can ship a dozen features in a quarter and have genuinely no idea whether a single one moved what customers care about.

That gap is where I spent a good chunk of my time at a food delivery business. The problem wasn't a missing dashboard — there were plenty. It was that no single number captured whether we were delivering the thing customers actually valued, in a way every team could be held to. So the work was to define that number — and then, the far harder part, to make the whole organisation actually steer by it. This is a walk through how you arrive at a metric like that, because the definition turns out to be the easy-sounding part, and almost none of the real work is in the definition.

The metrics we had were all rear-view mirrors

The numbers on every leadership deck were revenue, order volume, gross merchandise value. The trouble is they're all lagging — they report what already happened, and they can look perfectly healthy while the customer experience rots underneath them.

Picture a big, high-value order. In a revenue metric it's a great number this month. Now suppose it arrived at double the promised time. The revenue still booked, the dashboard still looked fine — and that customer quietly never ordered again. Multiply that across a city and you get a business whose lagging metrics look healthy right up until growth stalls and nobody can say why. What was missing was a leading indicator of customer experience: something that predicts whether people come back, and that every team can see themselves in.

It helps to be explicit about what such a metric has to be, because the criteria do most of the filtering. It has to represent something the customer genuinely values (not an internal vanity count like active users). It has to be a leading indicator of business results, not a lagging one. It has to be within reach of the teams' actual work. And it has to be legible — simple enough that anyone can read it and understand the strategy behind it. Most of the obvious candidates fail at least one of these on the spot.

Going back to the customer — and yes, it's obvious

So rather than mining the warehouse for something that correlated with revenue, I started from the customer. I partnered with the research team on a deliberately plain survey question: when you order food, what makes it a good experience?

The answer was strikingly consistent across every segment. Not the cheapest, not the flashiest app. Two things: they want it fast, and they want it when they were promised.

Now, an experienced leader reading this is entitled to a sceptical shrug: of course customers want food fast and on time — you didn't need a survey for that. That's fair, and it's worth meeting head-on, because the obviousness is not a weakness of the work; it's the reason the work was needed. Organisations drift. Under pressure to hit financial targets, teams quietly swap the customer's actual truth for internal proxies, and a dashboard can stay green while it stops meaning anything to a hungry person waiting for dinner. Going back to the obvious basics — and refusing to let them get abstracted away — is the harder discipline, not the lesser one.

And there's a real analytical step hiding inside the obvious. Stated preferences are cheap, so I validated the claim against behaviour: does wanting it "fast" and "on time" actually predict what customers do? I looked at repeat-order rate against actual delivery time and found a clear threshold — past a certain duration, repeat rates fall off, sharpest among the most frequent, highest-value customers. The same test on lateness against the promised time showed even a modest overrun measurably dented repeat behaviour in that same segment. Both effects were statistically significant. The obvious thing was true — but now it was demonstrated, with a threshold, rather than assumed.

That validation gave the metric its shape. I operationalised the two expectations into one composite: On-Time Fast Delivery — OTFD. An order either meets both conditions (delivered quickly and within its promised window) or it doesn't. One clean, binary flag, rolled up across every order: legible enough to explain in a sentence, anchored in what customers demonstrably value, and leading revenue rather than trailing it.

A metric you can't act on is just a poster

Here's where most metric work quietly dies: you land on an elegant number, put it on a wall, and nothing changes. A well-chosen North Star has a deliberate and slightly counter-intuitive property — you cannot move it directly. Nobody can go and "do some OTFD" on Monday morning. Its entire value comes from breaking it down into the input levers underneath, so each team can see the specific thing they control that rolls up into it.

So the larger half of the work was decomposition: walking the length of the order and mapping every stage that feeds OTFD.

The point of the tree isn't the boxes; it's that every leaf lands on a team that owns it. Waiting for food at the merchant is partner operations. Captain availability and fleet utilisation are supply. Time to assign is the dispatch and matching engine. Adherence depends on how accurate the prediction and routing models are. The moment the metric is decomposed this way, it stops being an abstraction on a leadership slide and becomes a set of concrete, owned jobs — each team can point to its leaf and know that's the thing it's accountable for.

Which lever first? Let the statistics decide

Twenty-odd input levers create an immediate problem: where do you actually invest? Every team will argue its lever matters most. Opinions are abundant and useless here; this is precisely where the analysis has to earn its keep and replace the argument with evidence. I approached it as two distinct statistical questions.

Stage one — sensitivity. How much does OTFD actually move when a given lever moves? I deliberately avoided raw correlation here, because many of these levers move together and correlation cheerfully over-credits them. Instead I used variance-based sensitivity analysis — Sobol indices and SHAP values alongside variance decomposition — to attribute how much of the swing in OTFD each lever is genuinely responsible for. That produces an honest ranking of impact rather than a ranking of what happens to be correlated with a busy month.

Stage two — distribution. A high-impact lever still tells you nothing about what kind of fix it needs, and this is the part I think most separates a rigorous analysis from a confident guess. For each lever I looked at two distributional measures across the population of orders and zones: the Gini coefficient — how spread out is performance? — and the Theil index — how bad are the worst performers, specifically? The combination sorts every lever into a quadrant. Low spread and low severity means the lever is consistent and probably already saturated — leave it alone. Low spread but a few severe outliers means a targeted fix on those bad actors. High spread with no catastrophic tail means a mass uplift across the board. High on both is the biggest opportunity of all: widespread and severe. A lever that's mediocre everywhere and a lever that's fine except for a handful of terrible zones might rank identically on impact, but they demand completely different interventions — and without the distributional view you'd treat them the same and waste the effort.

The output of the two stages together isn't a list of what matters — everyone already has opinions about that. It's a defensible order of operations: which levers to fund, in what order, and what type of intervention each one needs. That's the difference between an analysis that's interesting and one that redirects where an organisation spends its next quarter.

The real work was getting the org to adopt it

I want to be honest about this part, because it's the half analysts most often underestimate. Getting the framework right was maybe half the job. Getting the organisation to actually run on it was the other half, and it was harder.

You can't just publish a metric and expect teams to reorient. Every team already had local numbers it had optimised for years, and OTFD asked them to reprioritise around something new that, from their seat, felt abstract. What made it stick was translating the lever-to-team mapping into a concrete ask for each function. Partner operations didn't get a vague "improve OTFD" — they got a specific target on merchant prep delay, because the sensitivity analysis showed that was the lever they actually owned and that it mattered. Dispatch got assignment time. The mapping team got routing accuracy. Each team got the one thing it controlled, in its own language, with the analysis showing exactly how it rolled up into the North Star.

And it had to come from both directions at once. I walked each function's leadership through their specific lever and the evidence behind it — the bottom-up case. But I also took the whole framework up to product leadership in the quarterly planning forums, so it wasn't just me persuading individual teams; it became a top-down expectation reinforced from the top. That combination — every team seeing its own lever, and leadership treating the North Star as the number — is what turned it from a clever analysis into the way the organisation actually prioritised.

Why this is the hard part

A metric only becomes a North Star when people change what they do because of it — an organisational outcome, not an analytical one. It needs the analysis to be unarguable, the per-team asks to be concrete, and leadership to hold the line in planning. An analyst who can do the statistics but can't drive the adoption ends up with an elegant number nobody uses.

What a number like this leaves behind

The outcome was a single North Star the whole organisation could orient around — and every team's roadmap could be traced back to it. Instead of teams optimising in silos for their own local numbers, everyone could answer the same question: how does what I'm building make delivery more reliably fast and on time for the customer?

That's what I mean when I say this kind of work leaves a legacy. It wasn't a one-off analysis that shipped and was forgotten. It became the strategic backbone the organisation prioritised against — the shared language that turned a vague worry ("are we building the right things?") into a question with an evidence-backed answer rooted in what customers actually feel. Choosing the metric was, in the end, the easy part. Validating it against real customer behaviour, decomposing it into owned levers, ranking those levers with honest statistics, and getting an entire organisation to steer by the result — that was the work. And it's the part I'd point to as the difference between analysis that informs a decision and analysis that changes how an organisation decides.
