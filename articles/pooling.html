One Rider, Two Dinners: The Analytics Behind Pooled Delivery

How a delivery platform decides which orders should share a ride — and why getting it right is a measurement problem long before it's a product one.

Atif Salman  ·  Marketplace & Logistics Analytics · On-Demand Delivery  ·  July 2026

It's 8pm. Someone orders a burger. A few streets away, someone else orders dinner from the place next door. Both tap order within a couple of minutes of each other, both live in the same cluster of towers, and neither knows the other exists. Behind the screen, a decision gets made in seconds that neither of them will ever see: should these two dinners travel on one rider's bike, or two?

That decision is called pooling, and most of the time the customer never learns it happened. The food arrives. It was warm. As far as they're concerned, a rider brought their order — they don't know that same rider was also carrying somebody else's dinner, dropped a few minutes before or after theirs.

Pooling is one of the largest cost levers in last-mile delivery and one of the easiest to get quietly wrong. The reason it's hard isn't the routing maths. It's that every improvement you can make trades cost against customer experience, and you cannot manage that trade until you can measure it honestly. That measurement is the part I've spent my time on, and it's what this piece is really about.

Pooling, without the jargon

Strip away the operations language and pooling is a carpool for food. Instead of sending one rider per order, the platform occasionally hands a single rider two orders heading roughly the same way at roughly the same time, to be picked up and dropped off on one continuous trip.

There are two flavours. The simple one: both orders come from the same restaurant. The rider walks in once, collects two bags, delivers them to two nearby doors. The harder one: the two orders come from different restaurants that happen to sit close together, so the rider makes two quick pickups before heading out. Same idea, more moving parts.

That's the whole concept. Everything interesting is in deciding which orders should travel together — and in learning to measure the harm hiding in that second drop-off.

Which orders get pooled: two gates, and a simulator to tune them

Every order that could be pooled runs two gates. The first is eligibility: should this order even be a candidate? The second is pairing: given two eligible orders, can these two specific orders actually share a trip? Two words recur once you're inside the pairing gate, so it's worth fixing them now: the anchor is the order already in hand — the one a rider is being lined up for — and the candidate is a later order being weighed up to join it. Pairing always asks whether a candidate can be slotted onto an anchor's trip.

It also helps to separate two kinds of factor before listing them. Some are conditions of the marketplace we don't set directly — how dense the demand is in a zone right now, how short of riders it is. These decide whether good pooling is even on the table. The rest are thresholds we choose deliberately — how close two orders must sit in time, how far a detour may stretch — and they decide how close to that ceiling we run, and at what cost to the customer. The distinction matters because the two are improved in completely different ways, and confusing them is how a team ends up tuning a dial when it should be growing a zone.

The pairing gate is the one worth writing down. Four conditions, and a pair only survives if it clears all four: the two orders were placed close enough together in time, the detour is small enough, their promised delivery times line up, and the anchor isn't already so close to its own pickup that there's no time left to slot in a second one.

$$ \text{poolable}(a,b) \;=\; \bigl[\,|t_a - t_b| \le W\,\bigr]\;\wedge\;\bigl[\,\rho_{ab} \le \rho^{*}\,\bigr]\;\wedge\;\bigl[\,|p_a - p_b| \le \Delta p^{*}\,\bigr]\;\wedge\;\bigl[\,\tau^{\text{pick}}_a \ge \tau^{*}\,\bigr] $$
Order-time gap, detour ratio, promised-time gap, and the anchor's remaining time-to-pickup — all four must hold

The middle term is the one worth naming. The detour ratio asks how far the second stop drags the rider off the natural route — and it has to be computed independently for each candidate pair, never as a running total down a route.

$$ \rho_{ab} \;=\; \frac{d_{\text{pooled}}(a,b)}{d_{\text{solo}}(a) + d_{\text{solo}}(b)} $$
Detour ratio, computed per pair — the shared trip's distance against what the two solo trips would have cost

None of these thresholds — \(W\), \(\rho^{*}\), \(\Delta p^{*}\), \(\tau^{*}\) — is a number you pick on gut feel. To set them I built a simulator: it replays the eligibility and pairing logic over historical orders, so I can move any threshold and watch what share of eligible orders would end up pooled — the coverage — without shipping anything to a live customer. Every one of them in this piece was set that way: swept in the simulator, read off a curve, then confirmed against real behaviour. More on both steps later.

Demand density: the factor underneath all the others

Of the conditions we don't set directly, one sits underneath everything else and quietly decides how much good pooling is even on the table: demand density — how many orders land in the same zone within the same short window.

Density feeds both gates. At the eligibility gate, a near-empty zone has almost nothing to pool — there is simply rarely a second order to pair with, so the lone order isn't a missed opportunity, it never had a partner. At the pairing gate, density is what makes any given pair good: when many orders cluster in space and time, the closest compatible partner is genuinely close, so the detour is small and the added wait is short. Sparse density forces the opposite choice — reach for a far-away partner and accept a big detour, or don't pool at all.

The maths is unforgiving in a way that turns out to be useful. The number of candidate pairs in a window grows roughly with the square of the orders in it.

$$ \text{candidate pairs} \;\approx\; \binom{n}{2} \;=\; \frac{n(n-1)}{2}\;\sim\;\frac{n^{2}}{2} $$
\(n\) = orders in one zone-window; doubling \(n\) roughly quadruples the partners available — at no extra experience cost

That single fact reframes the highest-leverage move in the whole system. Loosening a threshold buys coverage, but pays for it in experience — you slide along the trade-off curve. Raising density buys coverage and lets you tighten the thresholds: smaller detours, tighter windows, and still more pooling than before. Density doesn't move you along the curve; it lifts the whole curve. It is the rare lever that improves both sides of the trade at once — which is exactly the definition of good pooling rather than more pooling.

And that is why pooling and Growth are not separate stories. The cleanest way to increase good pooling in a zone is to make that zone denser — acquire more customers and onboard more restaurants where demand already clusters, or is close to clustering. Marketing aimed at densifying those zones compounds in a way a pooling threshold never can: every new order raises the pool of potential partners for every existing order, a genuine network effect. A pooling team optimising alone is tuning dials on a fixed population; a pooling team working with Growth is enlarging the population the dials act on. The two should be reading the same zone map.

Density also has a geography and a clock. A tower cluster that's dense at 8pm is empty at 3pm, so the poolable population is a property of zone and window — the same \(z,t\) grain the marketplace-health index is computed on. There is no single citywide density any more than there is a single citywide stress level.

When riders are scarce, pooling leans in

When riders are scarce, pooling gets turned up, not down. It's one of the actions a stressed marketplace reaches for — and the companion piece on network health places it second on the response ladder.

The reason is what the alternative actually is. When orders outrun free riders, not pooling doesn't buy you a fast solo delivery — it buys you an order sitting in a queue with no rider to take it at all. Against that, putting two compatible orders on one scarce rider serves two customers instead of leaving one stranded. So as stress rises, pooling eligibility opens up rather than closing down: it becomes a lever for stretching supply exactly when supply is shortest.

That's why supply stress belongs in the eligibility gate as a live input, not a fixed setting. The same marketplace-health signal that tells the network it's short of riders is the signal that tells pooling to lean in — the two systems read the same number and move together, in the same direction.

Every threshold is a curve, not a switch

The underlying conditions set the ceiling. The thresholds we set decide where under that ceiling you operate — and here is the single most important thing I'd want anyone new to this to understand, because it's the reason "just pool more" is a trap.

Take any one threshold and loosen it. Coverage rises — but not linearly, and the experience cost rises faster the further you push. Plotted against how aggressively you pool, the savings curve bends early and flattens, while the experience-cost curve stays low and then accelerates. The job is not to maximise either one. It's to find the point where you've captured almost all of the saving while giving back almost none of the experience — the knee, just before harm takes off.

The two curves bend that way for a concrete reason, not because the picture is drawn to look tidy. When you first loosen a dial you capture the best pairs — the closest partners, the smallest detours — so saving arrives fast; every further step reaches for a worse partner than the last, so each increment adds less, and the curve flattens. Harm runs the opposite way: those first near-ideal pairs barely delay anyone, but pushing further starts admitting genuinely bad pairs — long detours, mismatched promise times — and a single badly-late second delivery costs far more than a marginal one, so the cost curve stays quiet and then climbs steeply.

It's worth being precise about what those two curves actually measure, because the whole optimisation rests on it. The saving is the delivery cost you avoid by having one rider carry two orders instead of dispatching two riders. The harm is everything that second drop-off can cost: the extra minutes the second-delivered order waits, the refunds those delays trigger, and the slower return — the customer who had a worse experience and comes back a little less often. The saving lands once, up front; the harm lands twice, first as refunds and then, later and quieter, as weaker retention.

Stated formally, the operating point isn't the maximum of either curve. It's where the marginal saving from pooling one more order stops outrunning the marginal harm it causes — with \(\lambda\) the weight the business is willing to place on experience against cost.

$$ \theta^{*} \;=\; \arg\max_{\theta}\;\Bigl[\, \text{saving}(\theta)\;-\;\lambda \cdot \text{harm}(\theta) \,\Bigr] $$
The threshold that maximises net value — not coverage, and not raw cost saving

Finding that knee for each dial — the timing window, the detour cap, the promised-time gap, the pickup-time cutoff — is most of the work, and it's where an analyst earns the right to say no. Some dials buy coverage cheaply, with almost no experience cost, and you turn those. Others look tempting on a spreadsheet and reliably produce late second deliveries the moment you relax them, and you leave those alone — and you can prove, with the curve, exactly why. The underlying conditions, meanwhile, don't move you along this curve at all; density and supply stress move the whole curve underneath it.

The word "pooled" hides two outcomes

There's a subtler trap, and it's the one that separates a real read from a naive one.

If you measure the experience of "pooled orders" as a single group, you get an average that hides the thing that matters. Because in every pooled pair, somebody is delivered second — and the second order is the one that rides the extra minutes, waits through the first drop-off, and lands late. Split the group by delivery position and the picture changes completely: the order delivered first looks almost identical to an order that was never pooled at all, while the order delivered second carries nearly all of the harm.

That single distinction reframes the whole strategy. The goal stops being "pool more orders" and becomes "pool far more of the orders that end up delivered first, and far fewer of the ones that end up a painful second." A pooling rate can't see that difference. A position-aware read can — and, as we'll see, it changes how you have to read an experiment, not just how you set strategy.

The control group decides the answer

One more piece of rigour, because it's where honest analysis and motivated analysis part ways — and it's really a question about the denominator.

Suppose you want to know what pooling does to delivery time. The tempting comparison is pooled orders against every other order on the platform. But "every other order" is stuffed with orders that were fast and simple precisely because they were never poolable — a lone order in an empty district was never going to share a ride with anyone. Against that easy backdrop, pooling looks like it inflicts a huge penalty. It doesn't. The right comparison is pooled orders against the orders that were genuinely comparable: the ones that cleared every filter and could have been pooled but weren't — the tightest causal control there is.

A useful analogy

Judging a carpool programme by what fraction of all drivers carpool is misleading — most drivers had no colleague going their way at their time. The honest denominator is drivers who could have shared a ride and didn't. Same discipline here: the only fair baseline for pooling is the set of orders that genuinely had a poolable partner, and a rate — or an effect — measured against anything wider is a mirage.

$$ \widehat{\tau} \;=\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\bigl[Y \mid \text{pooled}\bigr] - \mathbb{E}\bigl[Y \mid \text{eligible, not pooled}\bigr]}_{\text{like-for-like: the honest effect}} \;\ne\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\bigl[Y \mid \text{pooled}\bigr] - \mathbb{E}\bigl[Y \mid \text{everyone else}\bigr]}_{\text{naive: inflated by easy orders}} $$
The same question, two control groups — and two opposite conclusions

The payoff of that frame is that the conversation stops being "why is pooling only at X." It becomes: here are exactly the orders we're leaving unpooled, here is what it would cost in delays to capture them, here is the subset we can capture for almost nothing, and here is the subset we should never touch. That's a strategy a product team can act on — not a single number to argue over, but a map with the risk and the opportunity both drawn in.

Tuning the thresholds: simulate offline, then switchback online

The like-for-like control tells you what pooling did in the past. Setting the thresholds is a different question — what pooling would do at a setting you haven't shipped yet — and it takes two steps: a simulator to propose, an experiment to confirm.

Offline, the simulator proposes. It replays the eligibility and pairing logic over historical orders and, for each candidate value of a threshold, reports the coverage it would produce alongside what it would do to the second-delivered experience. Sweep the detour cap from tight to loose and you get the savings-versus-harm curve directly, order by real order. That gives a defensible starting point for every dial without touching a live customer — which is most of what the knee-finding in the last section actually rests on.

Online, the experiment confirms. A simulator replays the past; it can't see how riders and customers react when the rules actually change — whether a tighter window quietly reshuffles which orders get riders, whether a looser detour cap frustrates people into cancelling. So the proposed thresholds go into a live test. And the test has to be a switchback, not an ordinary user-split A/B, for a structural reason: pooling acts on a shared marketplace. Pooling one customer's order changes which riders and orders are available to everyone else in the zone, so you can't cleanly put one customer in "treatment" and their neighbour in "control" — their experiences aren't independent. A switchback sidesteps that by turning a threshold setting on and off for a whole zone over alternating time windows and comparing like windows against like.

Two metrics define success, and they have to move in the right relationship. The goal metric is coverage — more genuinely poolable orders actually pooled. The guardrail is delivery delay — how much later an order lands than the time it was promised — because coverage bought by pushing orders past their promise isn't a win, it's just cost quietly moved onto the customer. A setting only passes if coverage rises while delay holds.

Here the "pooled hides two outcomes" point comes back, and now it's an analysis rule, not just a strategy one. When you read the experiment you cannot score "pooled orders" as one bucket. A coverage gain that lands entirely on orders that end up delivered second — the ones carrying the harm — can look like a clean win on the blended average and be a real loss for the customers who felt it. So the guardrail is read position-aware: the delay on the second-delivered order specifically, not smeared across the pair.

And two things can still quietly distort the read, both worth naming out loud:

  • Selection between windows. Because a switchback compares whole zones across time windows, a window that happens to be busier or better-staffed can move coverage and delivery time for reasons that have nothing to do with the threshold. Balancing the windows and running enough alternations is what keeps that noise from masquerading as an effect.
  • Interference from the other stress actions. Pooling doesn't run alone. If demand-reshaping fires in the same zone during an "on" window, it pulls the serviceable radius inward and shrinks the very population of orders that were poolable in the first place. Because it fires on the same signal for on and off windows alike, it doesn't bias the headline — but it does mean the experiment is silent on the interaction: one action is quietly changing the other's raw material. Acknowledging that operational interference, not just checking for statistical bias, is the difference between a result that survives the second follow-up question and one that falls over on it.

The questions worth answering

Most of what I've described happens without the customer's knowledge, and the most interesting open questions are about whether it should — and about what the data can and can't yet tell us.

  • Should we simply ask? A saver delivery option — slightly slower, slightly cheaper, explicitly poolable — turns a hidden trade-off into the customer's own choice. It's also the cleanest experiment we could run: consent removes the guesswork about what people will tolerate. It could lift willingness to pool, or cannibalise full-price delivery. Worth testing, not assuming.
  • Should we tell customers their order is being pooled, and when? Telling them once a rider and route are locked may set expectations without alarming anyone; telling them too early, before anything is certain, just invites worry. The honest version is that we don't yet know the effect on trust or on repeat ordering — which is exactly why it deserves a measured trial rather than an opinion.
  • What do we owe the order delivered second? If pooling systematically asks one of two customers to wait, is it enough that it stays inside a promised window — or does the second customer deserve something back for absorbing a cost they never agreed to?
  • Should coverage flex with the health of the network? Pooling earns its keep when riders are scarce and matters far less when they're plentiful — which argues for eligibility that opens and closes with stress rather than a fixed setting. That's more complex to run and to measure. Is it worth it?
  • And the biggest lever of all sits outside the algorithm: if density is what makes good pooling possible, how much of the pooling roadmap is really a Growth roadmap — densifying the right zones — dressed in operations language?

Perfect pooling isn't the goal, and neither is maximum pooling. The goal is pooling the customer would have agreed to if we'd asked — enough to make the economics work, restrained enough that the person delivered second still orders again next week. Getting there is less about the algorithm than about who is willing to define the measurement, read it without flinching, and defend the trade-offs it reveals. That, more than any single model, is the job.
