Why Your Food Was Late — and Who the Data Blames

"Your order is running late" is the end of a story for the customer. For an analyst, it's the start of one: at which step did the clock slip, and whose step was it?

Atif Salman  ·  Marketplace & Logistics Analytics · On-Demand Delivery  ·  July 2026

You ordered at 8:02. The app promised 8:40. It's 9:05, the little bike icon hasn't moved in ten minutes, and you're refreshing the map like it owes you money. When the food finally lands, warm-ish, you've already formed a theory: the restaurant was slow, or the driver got lost, or the app just lied.

You might be right, but you have no way to know, and neither does anyone looking at a single number. "One in seven orders was late this month" tells an operations team nothing they can act on. Late where? Because the kitchen was slow, because no driver was free, or because the estimate was never realistic to begin with? Every one of those has a different fix and a different owner. Turning one blunt "late" into a specific, assignable cause is a measurement problem — and it's the one I want to walk through, because getting it right is far less obvious than it sounds.

The work didn't start with a model or a dashboard. It started by hand — pulling individual late orders and reading them one at a time: what happened at each step, where the time actually went, what the notes and the map showed. Doing that across enough orders is how the patterns first surfaced — the same failure modes recurring, the same stages quietly eating the clock. Only once those patterns were clear did it make sense to turn them into something systematic. The framework below is that manual detective work, generalised.

First, what does "late" even mean?

Before you can explain a late order you have to define one, and the honest definition is narrower than people assume. Late doesn't mean "took a long time." It means the order arrived after the delivery estimate the customer was shown. A twenty-minute delivery against a fifteen-minute promise is late. A fifty-five-minute delivery against a sixty-minute promise is on time. The customer's stopwatch started the moment they saw that estimate — so the promise is the yardstick, not the raw clock. That single choice quietly shapes everything that follows.

A late order is never just "late"

An order isn't a single event; it's a relay race through half a dozen stages, and the delay lives in one or more specific legs of it. The restaurant accepts the order. The kitchen prepares it. In parallel, a captain is assigned and rides to the restaurant. There's a handover. Then the trip to you, and finally the minutes spent finding your door.

Notice the parallel tracks before pickup. The kitchen prepping and the captain travelling in happen at the same time, and which one you're waiting on decides the whole story. If the food was ready and the captain hadn't arrived, that's a supply problem. If the captain was standing there and the food wasn't ready, that's a kitchen problem. The handover moment — who was waiting for whom — is one of the most diagnostic signals in the entire journey, and it's invisible unless you deliberately reconstruct it.

How the framework actually works, stage by stage

Here's the part that does the real work. The delivery estimate the customer sees isn't one number — it's built up from a separate prediction for each stage. One model predicts how long the merchant takes to accept, another the pickup phase — food prep and the captain getting to the restaurant — another the travel to the customer, another the wait at the door. Sum those predictions and you get the promise. That per-stage structure is what makes attribution possible.

For every stage, the framework then asks two questions in sequence. First: did the stage overrun its own prediction at all? If the food-prep model said fifteen minutes and it took sixteen, that's not worth chasing. But if it overran by more than a small tolerance, the stage is a candidate. Second — and this is the part that makes it fair — was that overrun outside the range this stage normally occupies? Not against a global average, but against the stage's own history for the same day of week and hour, over recent weeks.

The reason the second question matters so much is that stages are naturally variable, and a lot of that variability is expected. A kitchen that takes twenty minutes on a dead Tuesday afternoon and forty on a Friday dinner rush isn't "slow" on Friday — forty is its Friday-normal. Comparing every stage against its own distribution, rather than a flat citywide number, is what separates a genuine breakdown from ordinary noise.

That last branch — the benefit of the doubt — is deliberate. If a stage ran long but stayed inside its normal range, the delay is charged to the estimate, not the merchant or the captain. People are only blamed when they clearly break out of their own normal band. That principle isn't just fairness for its own sake: attributions drive real consequences downstream, from merchant scorecards to captain incentives, so the framework has to be conservative about pointing a finger, and honest about when the real culprit is a promise the system should never have made.

One delay, many possible culprits

The reason this is more than bookkeeping is that a single flagged stage usually has several possible underlying causes, and separating them is where the analysis earns its keep. "The captain arrived late" isn't a cause — it's a symptom with several very different explanations underneath it. Unpack the whole journey and one word, "late", fans out into a tree of specific, ownable failure modes.

Walk a few of the branches, because the distinctions are the whole point:

Captain arrived late — but why?

This one stage hides several stories. It could be supply stress — the marketplace simply had no free captain nearby when the order came in, which isn't the captain's failing at all. It could be low acceptance — captains were offered the order and declined it repeatedly, burning minutes before someone took it. It could be a captain who accepted but was slow to actually start riding toward the restaurant.

And there's a subtler one that's easy to miss: the dispatch system itself timed the assignment badly. Dispatch doesn't send a captain the moment an order comes in — it tries to time the assignment so the captain arrives roughly when the food is ready, which depends entirely on the food-prep prediction. So if that prep prediction is too long, dispatch deliberately holds the captain back, the food is actually ready earlier, and now the captain arrives "late" against a promise that was itself built on a bad estimate. It's a loop: a wrong prep prediction causes a late dispatch, which shows up as a captain-arrival delay that was never really the captain's fault. Untangling that requires reading the assignment timing against the actual food-ready moment, not just clocking when the captain showed up. Same symptom, four owners: the marketplace, the dispatch logic, the incentive system, and the individual captain.

The last mile ran long — routing, or the captain, or you?

A slow final leg splits the same way. If the captain's driving speed was within their normal range for that road and hour, the leg isn't their fault — the routing estimate was simply too optimistic about traffic, and the delay is charged to the ETA model. If their speed was abnormally slow, that's on the captain. And the very last stretch — the minutes at your door — has its own branches: a hard-to-find or wrong address, or a customer not answering, which is one of the few delays that genuinely sits with the customer rather than the platform.

The handover — a delay hiding in plain sight

The subtlest branch is the handover: cases where the food was ready and the captain had arrived, and there was still dead time between them. This is rarely a modelling problem — it's operational and data-quality reality on the merchant front. Sometimes the merchant marks the food "ready" well before it actually is, so the record says the captain dawdled when really they were waiting. Sometimes the captain is physically at the outlet but not attentive at the counter — not noticing the food is up, not collecting it promptly. These are messy, human, ground-level failures that a blunt "kitchen versus captain" split has nowhere to put — and that only a stage-aware framework, reading the handover moment directly, can even surface.

The whole point: each cause routes to a different fix

Everything above would be an academic exercise if it stopped at explanation. It doesn't — the reason attribution is worth this much rigour is that each branch of that cause tree points to a genuinely different lever, owned by a different team. A delay blamed on the kitchen and a delay blamed on routing have nothing to do with each other operationally, and lumping them under "late" guarantees you fix neither well.

The fixes split into two genuinely different kinds, and keeping them apart matters. Some are operational — they make orders genuinely faster: merchant governance to tighten prep, dispatch tuning to time assignments better, captain nudges to get riders moving. Others are estimation fixes — they don't change how fast the food arrives, they make the promise honest, so a delay that was really a bad estimate stops being counted as a fulfilment failure. Both reduce measured delay, but through completely different mechanisms: one changes reality, the other changes the promise to match it. Concretely, the branches map to distinct programmes:

  • Slow, inconsistent kitchens → structured merchant governance. Because delay is decided so heavily at the individual-outlet level, the highest-leverage fix is a governance programme: prep-time SLAs, periodic reviews, and targeting the specific worst-performing outlets rather than issuing a blanket city-wide policy.
  • Repeat-offender captains → captain performance governance. The same structural logic applies to the supply side: captains who show up consistently in the "slow to start" or "slow driving" branches, order after order, are a governance target in exactly the way problem outlets are — a named cohort for coaching or consequences, not a vague "captains are slow" complaint.
  • A prep delay happening right now → proactive care and live nudges. When the framework can flag an order that's cooking too slowly while it's still live, you can intervene in the moment — a nudge to the merchant, or a proactive message to the customer resetting the expectation before the promise is broken.
  • Captain late or not moving → dispatch tuning and captain nudges. Assignment-stage and dispatch-timing delays point at the dispatch and matching algorithms; a captain who accepted but hasn't started riding gets an automated nudge to get moving.
  • Routing and traffic misses → better last-mile ETA and routing models. When the delay is a genuinely too-optimistic travel estimate, the fix is model-side, not people-side.
  • Noisy stage timings → better ground-truth data. A lot of mis-attribution traces back to not knowing exactly when the captain arrived or the food was ready — so investing in cleaner signals (Bluetooth beacons at outlets, telematics on bikes) improves every attribution downstream.

That mapping is the real deliverable. The whole engine exists so that a single unhelpful word — "late" — becomes a routing table: this delay to merchant ops, that one to dispatch, this one to the routing model, that one to a data-quality investment. And it pays off: the interventions this attribution routed work to reduced delays by roughly 2 percentage points overall, and by as much as 3 points in the lunch and dinner peaks where the marketplace is most strained. The framework itself saves nothing — attribution is diagnosis, not treatment — but by pointing each team at the delays they could actually move, it's what made that reduction possible.

What none of this can do

The most important part of an analysis like this is the one that says where it stops — and there are real limits, at both the single-order and the whole-marketplace level.

  • It's a post-order framework by design, not a pre-order predictor. It explains what already happened; it doesn't forecast which orders will be late at checkout. That's intentional — most of what determines lateness is decided during fulfilment, in the actual prep, dispatch, and drive, not in attributes knowable before the order starts. The engine's job is the post-mortem, not the prophecy.
  • Every judgment inherits the ETA model's blind spots. The whole thing rests on the per-stage predictions being a fair source of truth — that's the starting assumption. If a stage's model is systematically off, the attribution built on top of it is off in the same direction: a chronically optimistic prep model, for instance, will keep charging delays to "the estimate" and quietly under-count genuine merchant slowness. The framework is only ever as honest as the predictions underneath it.
  • A clean attribution can still be the wrong story. Take a real case: an order lands an hour late, and the map shows the captain took a wildly abnormal time on the final leg — so the engine flags routing. Dig into the trip, though, and the customer had entered the wrong address; the captain drove to the first location, then re-routed. The stage genuinely was abnormal, but the true owner was a bad address, not the routing model. That's why the framework is a starting point, not a verdict — its real value is narrowing a million orders to the handful a human should actually read.
  • Some delays are simply unexplainable. A kitchen fire, a broken-down bike, a road accident, a sudden police closure — real, they make orders late, and none are predictable or preventable by better estimation. A share of delays will always be genuine chance, and a mature framework names that share rather than torturing the data to assign every last minute to someone.

Where this leaves the business

The customer sees one word — "late." Everything of value is in what that word hides: which stage slipped, whether it slipped outside its own normal range, which of several possible causes was really behind it, who owned that stage, and — across a whole marketplace — which structural pockets and compounding combinations drive the delay, and which team can actually move each one.

That is where this stops being reporting and becomes strategy. A framework like this doesn't just explain yesterday's orders; it becomes the structural backbone the delay roadmap is built on. It decides which teams get which goals — merchant ops owns prep governance, dispatch owns assignment timing, the ETA team owns recalibration — because the attribution is what proves those are genuinely separate problems with separate owners. It tells leadership where the next unit of investment buys the most reliability, whether that's a data-quality programme, a routing-model rebuild, or a captain-governance push. And it converts an emotional, argued-over metric into a shared, evidence-backed language for prioritisation. Building that — the definition precise enough to be fair, the rule conservative enough to be trusted, the model on top to find the shape of the problem, and the honesty about what none of it can explain — is where an analyst stops counting delays and starts deciding what the organisation does about them.
