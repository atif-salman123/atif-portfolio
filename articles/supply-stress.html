What Happens When a Delivery App Runs Out of Riders

On a Friday-night rush, a delivery platform makes a series of quiet decisions to keep dinner on time. Behind every one of them is a measurement problem an analyst had to solve first.

Atif Salman  ·  Marketplace & Logistics Analytics · On-Demand Delivery  ·  July 2026

Picture the fifteen minutes after Friday prayers let out, or the noon crush when three office towers order lunch inside the same ten minutes. Orders arrive in a wall. Every rider within a couple of kilometres is already mid-trip. And somewhere in the background, without anyone tapping a button, the app starts making decisions: it quietly pads the delivery estimate it shows the next customer, it starts putting two orders on one bike, it stops showing you the restaurant twenty minutes away, and — in the worst pockets of the city — it briefly stops taking orders at all.

None of that is improvised in the moment. It's a pre-built ladder of responses, and every rung on it depends on one deceptively hard question: how does the system know, right now, in this exact neighbourhood, that it has actually run short of riders — and not just that it's having a normal busy evening? Answer that badly and every clever response downstream fires at the wrong time. This piece is about answering it well, and it's a measurement problem long before it's a product one.

Supply stress, without the jargon

Strip away the operations language and the condition is simple: too many orders, not enough riders, right here, right now.

A useful analogy

Think of a call centre. Calls arrive; a limited pool of agents answers them. When calls come in faster than agents free up, a queue forms — and the longer it grows, the longer every new caller waits, until some simply hang up. A delivery marketplace is the same queue with wheels: orders are the incoming calls, riders are the agents, and when orders outpace free riders the "hold time" shows up as slow deliveries and, eventually, cancellations. The entire job is to notice that queue building early — and to know it's building here, in this neighbourhood, not on average across the city.

That "right here, right now" is what makes it hard. A city is never uniformly stressed — one district can be drowning while three streets away riders sit idle. Any single citywide number averages that away and tells nobody anything they can act on. Hold onto that point about geography — it comes back to shape almost every decision that follows.

So before you can respond to stress, you have to define it as a number. And that's genuinely contested, because both halves of the ratio have several plausible meanings. Demand could mean orders placed, orders still waiting for a rider, or orders not yet delivered. Supply could mean every rider logged in, or only those actually free to take a trip. Each pairing produces a different value — and a different answer to "are we stressed?"

Three tests before a metric earns the right to matter

Rather than pick a definition on instinct, the disciplined move is to decide up front what a good metric has to do, then hold every candidate against that bar. Three requirements earn their place.

The third test is the strict one. A stress metric is only useful if it moves with the thing you actually care about — the customer's experience, most directly their delivery time. Formally, that's just asking for a strong association between the two:

$$ \rho\big(\text{StressMetric},\ \text{DeliveryTime}\big)\ \longrightarrow\ \text{high} $$
A candidate only survives if this correlation is strong — otherwise it isn't measuring strain

Two candidates, one test — only one survives

Put two plausible definitions of stress side by side and hold each against the same experience signal. The intuitive one is unassigned orders divided by free riders — the backlog in the assignment queue over the riders idle enough to take it. It feels like the definition of strain. The quieter one is undelivered orders divided by active riders — everything the network is carrying, over everyone currently working it.

Plotted against delivery time, they behave completely differently. The intuitive metric is a cloud: it jumps around for reasons unrelated to real load, because it ignores every order already picked up and still on its way, so it's blind to the backlog the network has already committed to. The second is a tight, rising line — as it climbs, delivery time climbs with it. Only one of them earned the right to drive anything.

The definition that survived is worth naming. Call it the marketplace health index — undelivered orders over active riders. Written out, it's nothing exotic, and that's the point:

$$ \text{MHI}_{z,t} \;=\; \frac{\text{Undelivered orders}_{z,t}}{\text{Active riders}_{z,t}} $$
Computed per zone \(z\) and time window \(t\) — never as a single citywide figure

The reason it counts the whole pipeline is the same reason it stays stable: it sees committed-but-in-progress load that the queue-only proxy misses.

From one number to a ladder of actions

Once you can measure stress, the naive instinct is to bolt on a single lever: cross a line, do something. That's a mistake, because the honest response to rising stress isn't binary — it's a spectrum of actions, each costing the business and the customer a different amount. Jumping straight to the most drastic one throws away cheaper options that would have solved the problem first.

The better design escalates in proportion to the strain — a principle borrowed from engineering called graceful degradation: as load rises, a well-built system sheds capability gently and in order, rather than holding everything at full quality right up to the instant it collapses completely. Four actions, ordered strictly from cheapest to most severe.

Action one — inflate the delivery-time promise. Before touching supply or demand at all, show the customer a longer, more honest ETA up front, so the promise reflects a stressed reality rather than a number the system is about to break. It costs nothing but candour, and it heads off the most common complaint — an order that's late against a promise it never should have made.

Action two — pool orders. Put two compatible orders on a single rider — same or nearby kitchen, drop-offs close together — so a scarce rider serves two customers in one trip. Done well it stretches limited supply with barely any experience cost; done carelessly it makes the second customer wait. Deciding which orders are safe to pair is a measurement problem of its own, which I've written about separately.

Action three — reshape demand. Pull a merchant's serviceable radius inward so the system stops promising trips it can't reliably fulfil. The farthest orders are the first to go, and the size of the cut scales with how severe the stress is.

Action four — pause new demand. If nothing else is enough, stop accepting new orders in that pocket of the city entirely — the last resort, and the only action that forgoes real orders outright.

Every threshold is a curve, not a switch

The framework is the easy part. The analytical work is deciding, for each action, how aggressively to tune its threshold — how early in the build-up of stress it should fire, and how hard it should cut when it does. And to do that you have to be precise about what you're trading. On one side is conversion: an action that fires earlier or cuts harder slows or turns away more demand, so it suppresses growth. On the other is reliability — delivery time and delay rate — whose cost doesn't stop at the moment of a late order. A late delivery drives refunds now, and a customer who's had a slow, delayed order comes back less often, so the bill lands again later as weaker retention and repeat.

Take any one action on its own and picture tuning its threshold from gentle to aggressive. The reliability it protects rises quickly at first and then flattens — past a point, cutting harder buys you very little extra protection. The conversion you give up does the opposite: it stays low while you're only catching the genuinely bad cases, then accelerates as you start sacrificing demand that would have been fine. The right threshold for that action is the knee between them — almost all of the reliability gain, almost none of the conversion loss.

And this is where the shapes matter, because they aren't the same for every action — which is exactly why the ladder exists. Inflating the delivery-time promise barely dents conversion at any reasonable threshold, so it can fire early and cheaply. Pausing demand forfeits conversion outright, so its threshold sits far to the aggressive end, used only when nothing gentler will do. Each action has its own version of this curve, and its own knee.

Made precise, "find the knee" is just an optimisation. For each action, push its threshold to the point where the marginal reliability gained exactly equals the marginal conversion given up:

$$ \theta^{\*} \;=\; \arg\max_{\theta}\ \Big[\,\underbrace{\text{Reliability}(\theta)}_{\text{delays · delivery time}} \;-\; \lambda\,\underbrace{\text{ConversionLoss}(\theta)}_{\text{demand slowed / turned away}}\,\Big] \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \frac{\partial\,\text{Reliability}}{\partial \theta} \;=\; \lambda\,\frac{\partial\,\text{ConversionLoss}}{\partial \theta} $$
The weight \(\lambda\) is where business judgment enters — how much conversion you'll trade for a unit of reliability

Setting the thresholds: two passes, kept separate

In practice the thresholds get set in two distinct passes, and blurring them is a common failure.

Pass one is statistical. Plot the reliability metrics — delivery time, assignment time, delay rate — against the stress signal, and find the point where they bend: where a zone stops being merely busy and starts being genuinely bad. That inflection is tested for significance before it's trusted, so a real regime change isn't confused with noise. Each action's threshold is then anchored to one of these bends — the gentlest action to the earliest, the most severe to the latest.

Why every threshold is a per-zone number

None of this is set once for the city. A dense downtown district and a sparse residential one bend at completely different stress levels — the same index value that means "act now" in one zone means "still fine" in another. So each action's threshold isn't a single line but a set of zone-specific values, and the actioning itself is applied zone by zone: the app might be inflating ETAs in one district, reshaping demand in a second, and doing nothing at all three streets over — all at the same moment, all off the same metric read locally. Getting the geography wrong is how you either overreact in a zone that could have coped or underreact in one that's already breaking. The zonal grain isn't a refinement bolted on at the end; it's the unit the entire system operates on.

Pass two is judgment, and it isn't the analyst's alone. Statistically optimal isn't always operationally acceptable. A zone on the city's growing edge might warrant a more lenient threshold than the pure reliability data implies, because the business will trade a rougher near-term experience for longer-term expansion. That refinement comes from a genuine back-and-forth: the data proposes a defensible per-zone starting point, and the people who own that trade-off on the ground shape it into something deployable. Keeping the two passes visible means a deliberate business override never gets mistaken later for what the data said.

Testing a system, not a single lever

With thresholds in place, the question is whether they actually work — and this is where the experimentation gets genuinely subtle, because you're never testing one clean change in isolation.

The tests were run as switchbacks rather than ordinary user-split A/B tests, and that choice is forced by the problem. These actions act on a shared marketplace: reshaping demand or holding back orders changes the pool of riders and orders everyone in the zone draws from, so you can't cleanly put one customer in "treatment" and their neighbour in "control" — their experiences aren't independent. A switchback sidesteps that by turning the actioning on and off for a whole zone over alternating time windows, and comparing like windows against like:

$$ \widehat{\Delta} \;=\; \frac{1}{|W_{\text{on}}|}\sum_{w \in W_{\text{on}}} M_w \;-\; \frac{1}{|W_{\text{off}}|}\sum_{w \in W_{\text{off}}} M_w $$
Effect estimated by comparing "actioning on" windows against "actioning off" windows in the same zone

The design had just two kinds of metric, and the success condition tied them together. The goal metric was growth — conversion. The trade-off metric was reliability — delivery time, delay rate, no-rider cancellations. For the actioning to count as a success, the two had to move in the right relationship: conversion had to rise while reliability held steady or improved — or, for an action meant to protect experience, reliability had to improve while conversion held steady. A win on one bought at the cost of the other wasn't a win; it was just moving the problem.

Crucially, the experiments weren't only testing whether to act — they were testing where the thresholds should sit. The same switchback design was run across different candidate threshold values for an action, so the statistically-derived breakpoints from pass one were validated against live behaviour rather than trusted on historical correlation alone. The offline analysis proposed the threshold; the experiment confirmed it held up.

On the statistics: conversion, delivery time, and other continuous measures were compared with t-tests across the switchback windows; rate metrics like cancellation and delay were compared as proportions with a z-test. Because several actions were tuned across many windows, I treated the multiple-comparisons risk explicitly rather than reading significance off a dozen tests as if each stood alone.

The headline read, with the full ladder running against no actioning at all, was the one the design was built to produce: a clear lift in conversion with reliability holding steady rather than degrading. But a single topline invites a lazy interpretation, and the more interesting finding sat inside the cheapest action. Simply showing customers a more honest delivery estimate up front — before touching a single order — produced an outsized drop in delay-related complaints on its own. The least invasive action on the ladder did a disproportionate share of the experience-protecting work.

The interference that's easy to miss

There's one confound worth naming plainly, because it separates a careful read from a naive one. The actions don't politely wait their turn — more than one can be live in the same zone at the same time. Suppose you're switchback-testing pooling, and midway through an "on" window the stress climbs high enough that demand-reshaping kicks in too, on its own trigger.

Two things are true here, and you have to hold both. First, because reshaping fires on the same signal for the "on" and "off" windows alike, it doesn't bias the pooling comparison — it's layered on symmetrically. But second, it does mean the experiment is silent on the interaction: by shrinking the serviceable radius, reshaping quietly shrinks the population of orders that were poolable in the first place, so it changes pooling's raw material even when it doesn't distort the headline number. Acknowledging that operational interference — not just checking for statistical bias — is the difference between a result you can defend under questioning and one that falls over on the second follow-up.

Rollout: the dashboards, and why a human watches the top rung

A framework like this can't just be shipped and forgotten. Alongside the actioning I built the near-real-time monitoring dashboards and alerting that let operations and leadership see stress building zone by zone — demand, supply, the index, and the reliability metrics all in one place — with alerts firing the moment a zone approached a threshold, especially the point where demand would start being held back.

That monitoring layer isn't a nice-to-have, and the reason is unit economics. This entire framework sits on a live trade-off between conversion and reliability, and its most severe action — pausing demand in a pocket of the city — is a direct, immediate hit to growth in that moment. Leadership watches that trade-off closely precisely because it lands straight on unit economics: every order held back is revenue forgone, and every point of reliability protected has a cost. A decision that consequential can't be discovered in tomorrow's numbers; someone accountable has to see it coming in real time and be able to weigh it as it happens. The dashboards are what make an automated, self-escalating system safe to hand that much authority.

Where the analyst actually sits in this

None of these numbers set themselves — not the definition of stress, not the breakpoint in each zone, not the weight the business places on conversion against reliability, not the guardrails a conversion win isn't allowed to cross. The job wasn't to pick them by instinct. It was to build the frame that turns each one into a measured, defensible trade-off: three tests a metric has to earn before it's trusted, a definition that provably tracks the experience it protects, a ladder of actions ordered by real cost, a curve for every threshold, and an experimental design honest about what it does and doesn't isolate.

The payoff isn't a dashboard number everyone nods at. It's that when the Friday rush hits and the app starts quietly padding an estimate, sharing a rider, or narrowing a map — in this zone but not that one — every one of those moves has a tested reason behind it, and someone who can explain, without flinching, exactly why it fired when and where it did.
